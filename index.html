The Leap from 'Content Understanding' to 'Temporal Awareness'

We explores how to enable Speech Large Language Models (SpeechLLMs) to not only understand speech content but also precisely grasp its temporal structure, solving key pain points of traditional techniques with an innovative "relative timestamp" method.

The Problem: The Dilemma of Absolute Timestamps

Traditional methods rely on absolute timestamps but face three major challenges: vocabulary explosion, error accumulation, and poor generalization.

Traditional Method: Absolute Timestamps

Predicts the start and end time of each word as an absolute value.

Drawbacks
  • Requires a massive timestamp vocabulary for long audio.
  • Prediction errors accumulate and grow over time.
  • Struggles to generalize to time ranges beyond the training data.

Proposed Solution: Relative Timestamps

Predicts only the time interval between consecutive words, not their absolute positions.

Advantages
  • Small, fixed vocabulary, independent of audio length.
  • Effectively avoids error accumulation for more stable predictions.
  • Can process audio of any length by accumulating intervals.

Three Core Methodologies

The research adopts three key technical strategies to achieve efficient and accurate timestamp prediction.

Relative Timestamp Representation

Using inter-word intervals instead of absolute time points fundamentally solves the problems of vocabulary explosion and error accumulation, making the model lighter and more generalizable.

Hybrid Fine-Tuning Strategy

Full fine-tuning is applied to new timestamp-related modules, while LoRA is used for the original decoder layers. This balances learning new capabilities with preserving linguistic knowledge, achieving both efficiency and effectiveness.

Masked Timestamp Training

Randomly masking some timestamps during training forces the model to predict based on context and audio information, preventing overfitting and enhancing its robustness in real-world scenarios.
